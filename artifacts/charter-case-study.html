01 Volume, concentration, and a standing bench

Unlike a lean local shop that sponsors a role or two, Charter runs H-1B at scale from a single Denver-metro hub — and mostly renews the same workers year over year.

Of 134 certified Colorado filings, 118 sit in Greenwood Village (Charter's tech center) and 14 in Englewood. By employment type: 59 continuations versus just 4 brand-new — this is a permanent sponsored workforce being re-certified, not one-off hiring for a scarce skill. Titles are core engineering: Network Engineer, Systems Engineer, Software Engineer, Principal Engineer, Principal Architect.

03 Below-median levels & under-leveling

Beyond floor-hugging, Charter parks a third of its leveled Colorado roles in the two below-median wage tiers — and some senior-titled jobs are classified as entry-level.

Of the 83 Colorado filings that carry a wage level, 28 (34%) are Level I or II — the tiers set below the local median wage. And DOL's own guidance says "principal," "lead," and "senior" titles should trigger Level III+. Yet Charter filed "Principal Engineer I" at Level I and "Software Engineer V" at Level II — pushing the legal floor down. Absolute wages still span $78,900 to $214,698 (median $129,910); the issue is not poverty pay but that every figure is pinned to the lowest number the rules allow.

04 Stage two: the green-card pipeline

H-1B brings a worker in temporarily. PERM — the green-card labor certification — locks them in permanently, and legally requires the employer to first prove no qualified American is available. Charter runs this at scale in Colorado, with the recruitment red-flags critics describe.

To sponsor a green card, an employer must run a mandatory labor-market test — two Sunday newspaper ads, a state job order, internal posting — and certify it found no able, willing, qualified U.S. worker. The catch: the role is almost always already filled by the H-1B worker sitting in the seat. The search is a formality the employer must run but does not want to succeed. Two data signals track how that formality gets steered:

  • !
    Requirements that exceed the norm — 71%. Writing an unusual combination of skills or experience into the job description narrows the qualified pool toward the incumbent. It is legal if justified by business necessity, but it is the recognized vector for tailoring a role to one person. Seven in ten Charter Colorado PERMs carry the flag.
  • !
    Recruiting through the Denver Post print edition, atop layoffs. Charter's mandatory ads ran in the Denver Post (104 filings). Two Sunday print ads are legally required; the low-visibility print component is the concern sites like jobs.now surface — though, as noted below, Charter also posts these roles online. And 40 filings sit atop an attested area layoff — certifying "no American available" in a market where Charter had just cut jobs.
The two-stage cost play, quantified: Stage one, bring the worker in on H-1B at the wage floor (94% at the prevailing minimum). Stage two, convert to permanent via a recruitment process where 71% of roles carry above-norm requirements and the search "finds" no qualified American — for a seat that worker already holds.

The ad, in the wild

Pulled from the Denver Post, August 6, 2023 classifieds — one of Charter's ~19 recruitment ads in that single Sunday edition, rendered in telegraphic classified shorthand:

"Charter Communications, Inc. in Greenwood Village. Principal Engineer I … mintain & enhance telcommunications hrdware, SW … Salaries dep[end] on … exp., & edu.; negotia[tions begin at] $121,222/yr. Ref. co[de] …"

That $121,222 is the exact prevailing-wage floor on the matching DOL filing — the newspaper ad is the labor-market test for the floor-wage sponsorship.

What "browseable" reveals — a correction worth making: The same role is not hidden. Charter posts it on its ordinary careers site (e.g. live req 2026-76226, "Principal Engineer II — Multiple Openings," Greenwood Village), findable in normal job search, with the identical boilerplate — "negotiations begin at $174,068.76 per year" — and a stacked, hyper-specific requirement list ("10 years … 5 years … 2 years" of narrowly defined experience). So the mechanism is not concealment or antiquated mail-in applications. It is a browseable posting engineered by tailored requirements and a floor wage — legal, discoverable, and shaped so a local applicant is unlikely to qualify or bite. That is a more accurate, and more defensible, charge than "hidden jobs."

Honest limits: "requirements exceed the norm" is a scrutiny flag, legal when justified — not proof of fraud. Two Sunday print ads are the legally required method, not a Charter invention, and Charter does post these roles online where they can be found. The recruitment report (why each U.S. applicant was rejected) is not public. No worker is named — PERM discloses roles, not people. 07 Coverage, politics & lobbying

The floor-wage loophole is well documented in the abstract, and a live Senate bill takes direct aim at it. What's missing: any coverage naming Charter, and any Colorado voice on the fix.

Who's writing about it

The framework traces to the Economic Policy Institute's work by Daniel Costa and Ron Hira — the "60% below median" analysis of DOL wage levels. Trade press (Bloomberg Law, Forbes) covers the wage-rule fights. But coverage stays generic or fixed on big tech and outsourcers; a search found no reporting that names Charter on floor-wage H-1B, and none tying a specific Colorado employer's filings to its financials.

The bill written to stop this

The H-1B & L-1 Visa Reform Act of 2025 (S.2928, introduced Sept 29, 2025) would require paying the higher of the prevailing wage, the local area median, or the median Level-2 wage — which would end floor-hugging outright — plus mandatory U.S.-worker recruitment and a 30-day public posting. A March 2026 DOL proposed rule pushes the same lever administratively.

Colorado's absence: S.2928 has just five backers — Grassley (IA), Durbin (IL), Sanders (VT), Tuberville (AL), Blumenthal (CT). Neither Colorado senator is among them. Both lean the other way: Bennet (Gang-of-Eight) favors expanding high-skilled visas with wage protections; Hickenlooper favors streamlining and cap relief to fill labor shortages. The state with the sharpest floor-wage hub in this analysis has no delegate on the bill meant to fix it.

Does Charter lobby on it?

Charter is a heavy federal lobbyist — $10.9M in 2024, plus $1.5M to candidates — but its disclosed issue areas are Telecommunications, Broadcasting, Taxes, and Copyright. Immigration and Labor do not appear at all. Charter isn't defending the wage rules; it's a passive beneficiary of them. Notably, it also cut ~1,200 corporate jobs (Oct 2025) and ~1,000 call-center jobs (2024) while maintaining its Colorado H-1B engineering bench — different job categories, so suggestive rather than a clean link.

08 Verdict

✓
Can Charter afford local talent? Overwhelmingly. $5.1B profit, $4.3B free cash flow; the Colorado H-1B wage bill is ~0.4% of FCF. Cost is not the barrier.
!
Is the wage pattern cost-driven? Yes. Paying the exact prevailing minimum on 94% of roles, parking a third of leveled jobs below the median, and under-leveling senior titles is the textbook profile of using the wage rules to hold labor cost down.
!
Could U.S. workers plausibly fill these? The signal points that way. A standing bench of floor-wage engineering roles at one metro hub — renewed, not newly scarce — is exactly where paying market to recruit locally would be the expected alternative if the goal were talent rather than cost.
The honest limit: floor-wage sponsorship is legal, and wage level is a market-position proxy, not proof any specific American was passed over. Charter may have genuine reasons for individual hires. But where Ibotta showed affordability without a pattern, Charter shows affordability with the pattern — volume, floor-exact pay, below-median levels, and a standing bench — which is where the "could a citizen fill it" question has real force.

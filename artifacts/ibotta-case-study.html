01 The H-1B footprint is tiny

Across two fiscal years, Ibotta sponsored four H-1B roles — and two of those are the same Denver position. This is not a company leaning on the program.

03 The affordability case is airtight

The combined Denver H-1B payroll is 0.55% of one year's free cash flow and 0.1% of cash on hand. Paying a 20% premium above market across fifty senior engineering hires would cost ~$1.7M — under 3% of FCF.

FY2025 GAAP net income ($3.6M) reflects large one-time / stock-comp charges; adjusted net income ($51.4M, 15% margin) and free cash flow ($61.0M) better reflect the underlying cash economics.

04 Verdict: can-afford, yes — but a weak villain

✓
Can they afford local talent? Unambiguously yes. 86% gross margin, $349M cash, $61M annual free cash flow. Labor is discretionary spend for this business; the H-1B roles are a rounding error.
✓
Do they already hire local? Largely. 11 of 11 current engineering openings are in Denver, and the two H-1B seats are paid at the local market wage — one is simply a renewal of an existing employee.
!
Is this a displacement story? Not really. Two sponsored manager seats paid at median, against a Denver-first hiring pattern, is not the wage-arbitrage profile. The affordability point is real, but Ibotta is the wrong poster child.
The template lesson: "Can they afford it?" and "Are they abusing it?" are different questions. A rich company proves affordability trivially — but the abuse case needs volume + below-median wage levels. To see that pattern bite, point this same lens at a high-volume, floor-hugging filer.

01 The question, made measurable

"Could a U.S. worker fill this?" has no clean dataset — it's a judgment. But H-1B law forces every employer to hand over a strong proxy.

To sponsor an H-1B, an employer files a Labor Condition Application (LCA) and picks one of four prevailing-wage levels for the role. The level is supposed to track the skill and experience the job genuinely requires — and it maps directly onto where the pay sits in the local market for that occupation:

The program's stated purpose is to hire workers with specialized skills not available locally. If that were the norm, most roles would sit at Level 4. Instead, 60% of all H-1B jobs nationally are Level 1 or 2 — priced below what local workers in the same job already earn. A heavy tilt toward Levels 1&2 is the clearest public signal that a role is routine enough that a U.S. worker plausibly could fill it — and that wage savings, not scarcity, may be driving the hire.

Caveat up front: wage level is a proxy, not proof. It flags cost-driven, fill-from-the-local-pool roles; it can't confirm any single hire was avoidable. Per-employer wage-level shares below are national (EPI's analysis of FY2019 DOL data — the most granular public breakdown); Colorado filing counts and salaries are FY2025.

04 How to read it, by sector

IT servicesOutsourcing
The strongest "a citizen could do it" case. Firms like Tata, Wipro, Tech Mahindra, Mphasis, Infosys, Cognizant place staff at third-party client sites and price 78–98% of roles below median. This is the classic wage-arbitrage / labor-substitution pattern — documented cases exist of U.S. staff training their own H-1B replacements. Six such firms sit in Colorado's top 15.
Product techDirect hire
Mixed. Amazon and Microsoft price 77–86% of roles below median despite six-figure salaries — high pay, but low relative to the local going rate, so the routine-role signal holds. At the other end, Oracle (12%), Cisco (25%), Apple (34%), Intel (33%) put most roles at or above median — a profile more consistent with genuinely specialized hiring.
Higher ed & researchCap-exempt
A different animal. CU, CSU, Mines, DU, NREL, UCAR mostly sponsor faculty, post-docs, and PhD researchers — roles filled from a global academic labor market where the specific expertise, not the wage, drives the hire. They're cap-exempt (no lottery) and low-salary because academia is. The "displaces a citizen" frame fits this group weakly.
Other industryTelecom · finance · etc.
Charter and Dish are Colorado's two largest sponsors outright — telecom/network and software roles. Finance (Schwab, Visa, Western Union) and airlines (Frontier, high avg salary — likely pilots/specialists) round out the tail. Wage-level data isn't published per-firm for most of these, so they can't be scored on the same axis.

The honest bottom line

Wage level tells you where a role sits in the local pay distribution — a good proxy for how routine it is, and therefore how plausibly a local worker could fill it. It does not prove any individual hire displaced a specific American, and it can't see whether the employer ever advertised the job locally (most aren't required to). Treat a high below-median share as a red flag worth questioning, not a verdict. On that measure, Colorado's outsourcing filers raise the sharpest questions; its universities raise the fewest.

Method & sources

Colorado employer rankings, LCA counts, and average offered salaries are from the U.S. Department of Labor Office of Foreign Labor Certification (OFLC) LCA disclosure data for FY2025, as compiled by MyVisaJobs and cross-checked against h1bgrader's Colorado database (705 lifetime CO filers since FY2011). Per-employer wage-level distributions are from the Economic Policy Institute report "H-1B visas and prevailing wage levels" (Daniel Costa & Ron Hira, May 2020), analyzing USCIS Employer Data Hub and DOL LCA disclosure data for FY2019 — the most granular public per-employer wage-level breakdown available, national rather than Colorado-only.
